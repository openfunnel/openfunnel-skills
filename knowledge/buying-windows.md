# Buying Windows & The In-Market Problem


## Buying Windows

A buying window is the period when a company is most likely to purchase — the intersection of organizational need, budget availability, and decision-maker mandate.

### Types of Buying Windows

**Top-Down Buying Windows (easier to validate):**
- Relevant job posted → person hired for that role (2-month window)
- New executive hire with mandate to build something (90-day pressure to pick vendors)
- Board-level or C-suite announcement of new strategic initiative

**Bottom-Up Buying Windows (require deeper indexing):**
- Build-out signals — hiring manager posting 4+ roles for the same function
- First hire in a function — company entering a new domain, needs tooling
- Tech evaluation signals — team members actively comparing solutions

**Competitor-Triggered Windows:**
- Competitor renewal coming up → window to pitch alternative
- Competitor deal falling through → warm takeout opportunity
- Competitor engagement declining → dissatisfaction signal

### Why Buying Windows Matter

"Timing beats targeting. Every time. The best reps know this."

- The same company can be unreachable for 11 months and ready to buy in month 12.
- A person who just got hired to solve the problem you solve has budget, mandate, and 90-day pressure to pick vendors.
- Outbound relevance & timing >>> personalization. You don't need a better email template; you need to show up when they're ready.

**Key insight:** "Right people + right company is commoditized. Right timing is the only thing no one else sells."

## The "In-Market" Problem

### What In-Market Really Means

A company is "in-market" when there is a sense of organizational need — and need often emerges in time. But if you are building static lists, you aren't really there when the need comes.

**The stock market analogy:** Buying/selling stocks is based on revenue and P/E ratio, but there are also leading tips/inferences from tracking leading indicators that help you trigger. This piece was missing in GTM — reasoning capabilities across multiple distributed data points now make it possible.

### How Companies Signal Being In-Market

Companies give out distributed cues publicly that, when compressed and reasoned through, reveal stark insights about need:
- What they build (job postings, GitHub activity)
- What they adopt (tech stack changes, tool mentions in JDs)
- What they change (org restructuring, new departments)
- What they react to (competitor content engagement, industry event attendance)

### The Inference Gap

- LLMs are the best information gatherers and compressors. Previously, you had to outbound and find out if a company is in-market only through conversation.
- LLMs help make highly reasoned estimates about if a company is in-market — before the prospect even self-identifies.
- "The best leads are generated from meetups and sales leaders interacting with people with pain points" — the prospect was only relevant after the pain was known. Signal intelligence replicates this at scale.
- Due to lack of dynamic intel, people relied on static list outbound to gather information post-conversation. But the initial list is a massive waste of time if it doesn't have embedded insight.
